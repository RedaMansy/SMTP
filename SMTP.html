Secure Mail Transfer Protocol (SMTP)

SMTP is a TCP/IP protocol used in sending and receiving e-mail.

The majority of email systems that send mail over the Internet use SMTP to send messages from a server to another.
SMTP, however, is limited in its ability to to queue messages on the recieving end, therefore they are retrieved using POP or IMAP

History

At the time of its defintition by Jonathan Postel in 1892, the internet was minute compared to its vastness today.
It was merely a collection of military installations, universities as well as corporate research labs.
The number of hosts was so small, that all of the participants could recognize each other.
Due to the internet's size, there was further emphasis on reliability rather than security.
Users mostly configured their mail servers as open relays which meant that any of them could accept mail meant for other systems and pass it on until it reached its destination.

Protocol Overivew

SMTP is connection oriented and text based, meaning that a communication session needs to be established before any useful data can be transfered and it is in a human readable format that does not contain a gui. The mail sender communicates with a mail receiver through certain commands and through the supply of necessary data over a data stream channel, usually TCP. A standard SMTP session consists of commands originated by an SMTP client and the corresponding responses from the SMTP server. An SMTP transaction consists of three command/reply sequences:

MAIL

This establishes the return address.

RCPT

This establishes a recipient of the message.
It can be issued multiple times for each recipient.

DATA

This signals the beginning of the message text.
It also contains a message header and a message body.
